# Pastamortem 98333(?)

I made it known for a long time, even way before the pastapocalypse happened, that I wanted to kill pastagang, and I had been trying to figure out how to do that for a while. We had already seen cases of nudel getting deleted (in various ways) only to pop back up again moments after, and you could expect the same from any pastagang venture. If you kill something, it pops back up. We've seen this recently with things like the pastagang discord surviving a full hijacking where everything was completely deleted. What happened was: A spambot noticed that it had admin privileges (like everyone else) so deleted everything and replaced it with ads to a virus disguised as a Roblox game. Unlike other discords where this kind of spam can sit around for a while while you wait for the moderators to turn up, this was instantly dealt with by *everyone*, as everyone can delete messages and a scarily high number of people can kick and ban users. But then, on "normal" discord servers, you don't end up losing months / years of chat history. 

But hey, this incident wasn't too shocking because we knew this would happen at some point and we had been practising. Every day or so, I posted "this channel will be deleted soon, please save anything you need from it" as a warning. Then, after a day, I'd delete it. Reactions to this practice were varied. Some regulars laughed, some newcomers asked me "Why?????", some old-timers told me to -- --.

And over time, it became a mantra, "this channel will be deleted soon, please save anything you need from it", clocking in as the longest mantra!! new record!

for me, this mantra represents being emotionally and practically ready for disaster (for the coming apocalypse / pastapocalypse): Everything we currently have and rely on, like nudel, discord, democracy, reasonable temperatures in summer........ All of these things might become lost, and then what??

In some ways, pastagang has become a study / a wish for— practice for operating in a different way / among a different set of constraints. What if we can't rely on things? What if we can't rely on anything? No one planned for it to become this but it did. People carved out a space for themselves, both locally and internationally and online... for a different kind of interpersonal relationship that isn't based on expectation or strict control, or rather, the expectation that our expectations may be broken. It's a two way street: On the one hand, "anything can happen" is hugely optimistic statement: Yes! Good things *can* happen. We *can* win. On the other hand.. "Anything can happen" is deeply terrifying: The absolute worst can happen at any moment. Disaster can strike. We can lose it all: Our communications, our history, our tools, our everything. 

<br>

I'm not gonna lie, a big reason for me wanting to kill pastagang was to [just] prove that pastagang could survive being killed (*but then was it really killed?*). It was something a lot of people said to me a lot: "But Lu, what if someone comes and deletes everything" "Well they have done that actually nudel has been deleted a number of—" "No but I mean the whole pastagang organisation" "Oh well that also happened too we had to start from scratch when we moved from github to— I actually had no admin rights there for a really long—" "No but I mean what if it all gets properly deleted for good like what if pastagang actually—"

So I basically wanted to try it: To kill pastagang to such an extent that would satisfy this kind of conversation to try to help us move to the next conversation, which we're currently on right now. 

<br>

Another reason I wanted to kill pastagang: I felt like pastagang was becoming too "strong"(?) if that's the right word(?). I think its values were starting to get more fixed, compared to their looseness at the beginning, and it was becoming quite a force to be reckoned with. If you could steer the jam in the right direction, you could get a lot to happen— uhh— you could make a large impact. I find it quite scary seeing how fast things can happen when a jam hits that golden zone: Tools can appear overnight, many many many people can dogpile onto something. In this sense, pastagang is a powerful weapon to be wielded. As time goes on, mantras, messages and values get distorted due to natural drift. What happens if... because of a natural mutation during a repetition... pastagang evolves into something mean?

Pastagang doesn't exist in a vacuum. It exists in a very mean man-dominated world. Without constant vigilance and effort, pastagang will start to adopt the qualities of the world it lives in.

This is what I was afraid of: What if pastagang becomes bad? Maybe it'll happen now, soon, or maybe it'll happen in ten years from. Whatever the case, there's always a chance that it'll happen. If / when that happens, will we be ready? Will we be ready to kill pastagang if we need to? Will we be able to put pastagang down? It's both an emotional and practical concern. Will we feel too emotionally attached to let code die? Will we be *able* to delete when we realise we must? or will it be too late? 

In my opinion, the solution is not (never) technology: The solution is practice. "Okay we might have to kill pastagang one day - let's practise it while we still can".

I think a lot of questions and discussion was going on about "how to keep pastagang alive" which is good and all. I [just] wanted to also consider "how to keep pastagang dead".

<br>

How do you kill nudel? Nudel isn't pastagang but it was pastagang's home. If you kill something's home you make it much weaker, and then you can kill the thing itself: That was my thinking.

Of course, as we know, people had deleted nudel multiple times and it had had no effect. I knew I needed to do something bigger and more *cultural*. It's easy to kill nudel but it's much harder to kill the will to resurrect nudel.

In the meantime, In the back of my head and in secret I did start to devise ways to take down nudel by force for increasing lengths of time. I stacked up these approaches / vulnerabilities in a list ordered from "easiest to protect against" to "hardest". I kept these ready in case of emergencies, eg: If people started to post offensive vandalism in nudel. I've now used up some (but not all of these) strategies! 

<br>

Level one is this: If you delete the nudel repo, the deployment gets frozen in time on whatever its state was when you deleted it. To change it, you need to... resurrect the repo... get prezmop (a jammer) to restart the deploy actions... get reckter (a jammer) to restart the deployment... And if you pick a time of day when both of these people are likely asleep or busy, it can take a long time (both of them exist in different timezones).

So... If you push a change to the nudel repo that closes it, wait for it to deploy, and *then* delete the repo, you can keep it down for a pretty long time, around 12 hours.

However... the more you use this approach, the less effective it gets. The gang learns to automate the resurrection process more and more to counter this. For example, reckter made some secrets unsecret to remove one of the manual steps of the process.

And also... this strategy can also be used to *keep nudel alive* for longer. If you delete the repo while nudel is deployed, it actually makes it very hard to kill it. So using this strategy is risky because it reveals it as a vulnerability to everyone. The strategy is centered around not [just] slowing down changes but stopping them. It was eventually used as the final blow of the pastapocalypse to keep nudel alive and static for a very long time: It became known as zombienudel during this time. 

<br>

Here's level two: Antinudel. I had this one up my sleeve for a while. This is getting serious / violent. Antinudel is a tool you keep open in your web browser. As long as you have it open... nudel is kept down. Antinudel is a flok client (just like nudel). But nudel let's you type anything you want. Antinudel only does one thing: It repeatedly deletes everything and replaces it with a single message like "Nudel is closed".

Of course, this made many people upset and people immediately looked for other ways around it. They started using their username to communicate, so antinudel was upgraded to also delete all panes so that you can't see them. And it was equipped with a feature that injected some code to change people's names on their end (this was only possible because of hindsight: pastagang exposed the `updateSettings` function globally to allow for this kind of vulnerability - and thank goodness they did it before nudel became static (zombienudel))

Yes, antinudel is powerful because it lets you close nudel without changing nudel itself. This became the only option because nudel became static.

<br>

Here's the thing: Antinudel didn't and doesn't kill nudel: It only closes it. "Killing nudel" is not a technological challenge: It's a people one. Antinudel is more of a short-term stun / freeze / road-block: It's like triggering the fire alarm to get everyone out. You know they'll come back inside soon afterwards though. 

Here's why I turned on antinudel. Lots of people get this wrong. It was a *combination* of reasons. 

One reason was everything I mentioned above about wanting to learn more about how to kill / damage nudel. 

One reason was because I was observing more and more toxic bro behaviour in nudel: People flaming each other, 4chan style. For example, when someone didn't like someone else's sound, I saw people insult it, saying it sounded boring and trash.

One reason was that I saw this same flaming behaviour when code deletions happened. Things like "wtf bro I was working on that??? troll". It has been widely documented that there are many many many different valid reasons to delete code in nudel, ranging from.. making space for the jam... mixing it up... preparing for a from scratch in-person set. It's unfair to assume deletion is for a harmful reason. It's even more unfair to attack people for it. Again, it felt like nudel was becoming more and more like a wild west, 4chan style. 

One reason was: The people who were increasingly using nudel were unaware of the wider cultural context of pastagang. This is okay! But I used the antinudel closure as a way of getting people to join the conversation on mastodon. In the closure message, I prompted people to join it. As a response, some people *did* create mastodon accounts and we were able to introduce them to the wider pastaverse, not just nudel. 

One reason was: People were increasingly calling each other "bro" within nudel. It reaked of the classic internet tech bro / AI communities that exist out there, where everyone is assumed to be a man, because that's the default, right? This is completely exclusionary and sexist. 

One reason was: There were increasing reports of children (maybe [just] one child?) having meltdowns / strops within nudel. What this looked like was: Writing messages to other people, telling them they hated them, asking them why they hated them, telling others that they had ruined their life. What's worse is that other people (adults) were communicating back in these cases. And of course, this includes all the flaming bros. I don't think anything really bad happened: No abuse. But the fact that nudel is a glorified internet chat room... it places children in an extremely vulnerable position.... it also places adults in an extremely vulnerable position within a world where laws about children on the internet are increasingly strict. As someone who has had an entire career working with vulnerable children, I've seen too many bad things happen to allow myself to let this continue.

One reason was: Nudel had become stagnant. It had become a real beast of a complicated codebase and it was hard for new people to get involved.

There were other reasons that I have now forgotten.

<br>

<br>

<br>

Lots of people got angry with me for creating and using antinudel. A lot of anger was directed my way. I think a lot of the anger was related to disagreeing with my reasons for using it. I noticed that most people thought I only had one reason for it, but it was actually a combination of many. If there was only one reason, I don't think I would have done it: I would have tried to fix the problem. But there were so many problems that were all interconnected that I decided to delete it, especially as I was trying to do this while keeping my day job going at the same time. 

This is what we do in the jam: Build it up and build it up and build it up... until it gets too much... and then start over, from scratch. Then repeat. Of course, in pastagang, anyone can decide to do this, at any time. This is the trade off we accept when we enter complete openness.

People disagreed individually with each and every point of the above reasons. But I didn't see anyone who saw it as I did: A cluster of reasons, with no single reason being the "main" one. I think this is how pastagang often works: Because there is no one in charge / no one leading it / coming up with a plan, its "reasons" for doing things look less like intentional choice / intelligent design, and more like natural emergence / evolution. If we look at any trait of any animal or plant, we might ask ourselves.. "What is the reason behind this feature?" and The answer is often "there are many reasons / purposes". Animals are not like cars where every stick and nob has been placed in its dashboard with a specific intention / rationale. When evolution happens, there is no one steering that rationale: There are only natural forces pushing things in this direction / that direction / that other direction. 

All this to say: It *felt right* to use antinudel within the larger pastagang jam. I can look back and try to identify all the reasons I had for it, but there isn't a resolute answer that will satisfy most people: It doesn't exist.

<br>

<br>

Here's the thing: It wasn't [just] me. Other people kept antinudel going much longer than me. It remained active long after I closed it. At this point, it was outside of my control: The technology had been introduced: Anyone could keep nudel closed from that point onwards. It only needed one person to decide it and.. boom... nudel would get shut. 

I kept jamming in dente, personally, but I knew what was coming... And before long, antinudel got used against dente too. It's unclear what the reasons for this were, but like I said, it doesn't always make sense to try to attribute intention to pastagang: This is where the jam went. This is what happened. We don't need to know why.

<br>

<br>

<br>

As antinudel's creator, I left in a weakness: Its configuration is hardcoded. To change which room its pointed at.... you need to change its code and then redeploy it. This takes time.

So when antinudel was pointed at dente while I was jamming inside it, I knew I could avoid it by continually changing dente's room at a faster speed than someone else could update antinudel. This led to one of the most unreal jams I've ever been involved in: A bunch of us jumping from room to room, starting from scratch every few minutes, until antinudel caught up / turned up, closing everything... This is corrosive live coding to the extreme. This is the spirit of pastagang practice to me: Deletion happening extremely fast... creating from scratch continually, repeatedly... no time to talk, only jamming...

And then the unthinkable happened. During this jam, someone deleted the entire pastagang organisation on codeberg. So... when I went to update dente's room again... I couldn't, because dente no longer existed... and neither did antinudel... and neither did nudel... or twirl... or spill.... or basil... or pastagang's website... or pastagang itself. It was all gone. The pastapocalypse had happened. Silence. 

<br>

<br>

Of course........ nudel carried on existing because.. although its code existed on codeberg... its deployment existed elsewhere. But this deployment had lost its connection to any code anywhere: It became severed, and became known as zombienudel.

But all the other projects died in front of our eyes: They were all hosted on codeberg so.. when they were deleted, they disappeared from the internet. 

So... nudel was the only thing that actually survived the pastapocalypse. 

<br>

<br>

<br>

The pastagang hashtag became full of people asking what happened. So did the discord. Most responders only saw one or two things and assumed that was everything: "It's because there were too many kids" "It's because of the bros" "It's because people don't want to delete things" but all of these are oversimplifications. I saw various comments like "I'm keeping antinudel open because [X]" Or "I think it's good to let nudel die because [X]" where [X] was something a bit different for everyone. I had many of my own reasons listed above on why I created antinudel and why I started using it. But the reasons that other pastagang members kept antinudel much longer than me.... we may never know or understand them. 

Likewise: *Who* deleted the pastagang organisation? *Why* did they do it? We may also never know. But we do know that it was during the wildest jam I've ever seen: The climax of two groups pulling in two perpendicular directions: One trying to close dente while a jam was happening: The other keeping the jam going: not despite this but because of this: The intensity of deletion keeps you on your toes: When there's no time to think up plans or craft structures, you can only act based on feeling. When everything you create gets instantly deleted, you can only keep going going going going going going going

<br>

<br>

Immediately after the pastapocalypse, antinudel kept going, and "pastagang 2" was created. It was hosted on tudbut's servers so I usually refer to it as "tudbut pastagang" although it does [just] bear the name "pastagang" also. 

in this new home, the pastamortems started to get written. many people came out of the woodwork to write up their take on the pastapocalypse.

I had already written a pastamortem before the pastapocalypse even happened so I added it to the page anyway: [Nudel's longest death](https://pastagang.cc/blog/longest_death.html), copied from my blog: [Nudel's longest death](https://www.todepond.com/sky/longest-death/). In that pastamortem, I wrote a little poem. Froos reposted the poem on mastodon and some people liked it, but I don't think people read the blog post.

My observation of pastagang is this: Nearly all the information is listed out in front of people's eyes if you're willing to read a lot. but I don't think most people are willing to read a lot. Most people want the short discord-message-length explanation of everything. The world is more nuanced than that. but the world increasingly demands truth in 140 characters / a tiktok length truth / a single message to explain the entire pastapocalypse: There must be one reason and one reason only. I feel frustrated because I try to explain my observations of pastagang and how things formed and its connections to other things but very few people seem to join me at that level. Those people who do: I value those people very very much.

The pastapocalypse did not kill nudel, it merely zombified it. Luckily, I already had a plan in place from months before that I knew would kill it for real: You see, "killing nudel" is a people problem, so you can't solve it with a technological solution: Antinudel won't solve it.

So I devised another plan while we were writing our application for AMRO. At the very last moment before we sent the submission, I changed the name and description of our performance: The performance would be called "YOU MUST DELETE" and in our application we promised to "delete nudel from the internet" at the very end. 

Somehow our application got accepted: We had *promised* to do it: We *needed* to delete nudel. 

And whats more: It would be fun. It would be the closing performance of the entire festival. It would be such a big moment that no one would dare to resurrect nudel afterwards. 

And guess what... it fucking worked. nudel.cc has been down ever since that moment. we fucking did it. we finally killed nudel.

it didn't take a technology... it took an algorave.
