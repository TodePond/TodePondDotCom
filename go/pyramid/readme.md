I'm quite inspired by bands like Wet Leg(!) where different band members have very different roles in terms of stage presence / presentation. a more visible person, a more hidden person: but both styles feel celebrated and intentional and owned. or Confidence Man where two band members always where veils. or Slow Magic's mask. or the Gorillaz of course. there's something celebratory / honest about the manufacturing / hiding

i think there are many reasons why someone might want to hide their face and i don't need to know which one it is. all i need to know is what kind of vibe to go for

when editing the first video (and the second one, to be honest), i kept getting worried about where to land on the "intentionality" scale. or maybe i phrased that badly. i wanted the hiding to look intentional / deliberate. i wanted it to not be a secret. i didn't want it to be distracting. i didn't want it to look sloppy. i wanted it to feel like care was involved. i didn't want it to be perfect. some of these things might sound a bit contradictory, like "not sloppy" and "not perfect" but i think they actually go together kinda well: when something feels handmade it definitely isn't perfect but it definitely is carefully done: it still gets your fingerprint / handwriting regardless of how meticulous you are

my camera picks up very bassy audio so it always works well too combine it with more tinny audio, eg: from the livestream in this case. 

at first i was a bit confused about why the combined audio sounded worse in some places. after a lot of listening and sanity checking i noticed that the livestream audio shifted slightly forwards and backwards at certain points. weirdly it happened whenever the music got louder. so it wasn't just a case of making sure the two audio tracks were synced up: I also had to change their offsets at various points throughout the video. once i figured that out and did that it sounded so much better and i was really pleased. 

before i figured it out, i was considering mixing in some audio from some of the phone footage for the important bits, and i tried it out! but I'm pleased i didn't have to do that because it sounded way less bassy

i start by editing the whole thing. i go through in multicam editor mode, picking out which shots to show when, without caring too much about timing and pacing. i mainly pick out the interesting things I want to show. then i go back through, having seen the whole thing, trying to carve out more of a progression. i think about scaling up and scaling down the rate at which cuts happen. and i think about which angles are precious / more high energy, and try to create a feeling of "unlocking" them as time goes on. 

i feel like i could talk for years about editing and trying to create stories through editing. it's really my most favourite artform. don't think i have room for it here

later on, i go through, tweaking the timing of cuts to make things feel right. there are a lot of decisions about whether the cut happens before, after, or during a sound. a change of 50 milliseconds can make a huge difference. of course, i don't know if the viewer will be watching on laptop speakers or wired headphones or wireless headphones so i don't know what the timing will actually be. i like to edit for really low latency speakers because i find that that results in something that works for high latency speakers (like Bluetooth headphones) but not the other way round.

either way, the relative change between cut-to-beat offset is always noticeable. i can make one part feel more sluggish by making the cuts come later. i can make one part sound more "steppy" by bringing the cut earlier.

i really like to include the long setup time in these supercuts. i want it to feel less mysterious / more tangible / human by showing us struggling to plug in and whatnot at the start. i also like to show the long long build-up to the climaxes of the performance, and that build-up *does* include setup time. 

including this slow start is terrible practice for youtube videos because the algorithm really values the very beginning of the video. but making a video i like is more important than that. 

i also know that posting cuts of performances on my channel always does really badly compared to other kinds of videos, but i also think it's more important to put out things i like. 

for me, it does make sense in my "brand" to put out things that are a little obtuse / hard to get into. i want the long build-up at the start of the video to cut out the unkeen, and i want there to be rewards later in the video for those who stay, so that they learn that yes, it is worth sticking around for a todepond video: even through the boring bits. 

thanks to nadya showing me Ena stuff, and through remembering some of my roots, I'm rediscovering that it's okay to put out things that don't make sense to people, and i dipped my toes into that right at the end of this video with the pyramid thing. i think there's something really fun in confidently not-explaining-yourself and [just] doing what you want to do.
